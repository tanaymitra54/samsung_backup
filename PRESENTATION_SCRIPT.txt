Presentation Script (approx. 8-10 minutes)

Good [morning/afternoon] everyone.

Today I will explain our Samsung PRISM project in a very simple way: what problem we are solving, what existing research does, what gaps we found, how our method is different, and how much improvement we are targeting.

First, the problem.
Small language models are efficient and cheaper to run, but they often struggle in multi-step reasoning, such as math word problems and logic tasks. If the model makes an error in step 1 or step 2, the final answer is usually wrong.

Now, what do standard methods do?
Chain-of-Thought asks the model to reason step by step. Self-Consistency generates many chains and takes majority vote. Tree-of-Thought explores branches. Combinatorial Reasoning and QCR-LLM use optimization approaches.

These are strong methods, but they still have practical shortcomings for our target.
Some need very large models. Some are expensive because they sample too many paths. Some rely on voting, which can still select common but wrong answers. QUBO-based methods in prior work are mostly tested on larger models and larger optimization size, and quantum-first methods can depend on special hardware.

So what did we do differently?
Our approach is a three-stage pipeline:

Stage 1: Sampling.
We generate multiple reasoning candidates with diversity controls, so we do not get near-duplicate reasoning.

Stage 2: QUBO optimization.
We convert candidate reasons into an optimization problem. The solver selects a subset that is both relevant and non-redundant.

Stage 3: Final inference.
We feed only the selected best reasons back to the model and get the final answer.

The key novelty is this:
We added correctness-aware scoring before QUBO selection. Earlier methods mostly use similarity and redundancy. We additionally score whether a reason is likely correct, for example with rule-based checking for math and lightweight NLI for commonsense. This reduces hallucinated reasoning entering the final selection.

Our second major improvement:
We adapt QUBO specifically for 3B-class small models by semantic clustering, reducing variable count from around 900 in prior large-model settings to around 100 to 200. This makes the approach practical on CPU and A100.

Third improvement:
We include QUBO-based hyperparameter search, which jointly tunes settings like temperature and sample counts, instead of slow manual or grid tuning.

Fourth and future-facing improvement:
We plan HUBO and optimized annealing schedules, plus a QUBO-guided fine-tuning loop so the model itself learns from high-quality selected traces.

Now, the expected impact.
From our roadmap, projected GSM8K progression is roughly:
60 percent baseline,
66 with CoT,
72 with diverse sampling,
78 with QUBO selection,
84 after correctness-aware verifier,
88 after QUBO-guided fine-tuning,
and about 91 with optimized annealing plus HUBO.

So overall, the projected gain is about plus 31 points from baseline, and this exceeds our defined 2x target trajectory.

Why is this useful in practice?
Because we are trying to get near large-model reasoning quality while staying in a small-model deployment budget. That means lower cost and better deployability, without giving up too much reasoning accuracy.

To conclude:
Our work stands at the intersection of AI reasoning and combinatorial optimization.
Compared to the papers, our contribution is not just using QUBO, but making it SLM-ready, correctness-aware, and deployment-oriented.

Thank you. I am happy to take questions.
